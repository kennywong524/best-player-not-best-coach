The Best Player Is Not Always the Best Coach: Benchmarking LLMs as Workers and Assistants Across Professional Tasks

Pattaraphon Kenny Wongchamcharoen, Kris Gulati, Min Min Fong, Abhishek Nagaraj · Data Innovation and AI Lab, Haas School of Business, UC Berkeley

Project overview

Which model can improve the work of another?

Most benchmarks score a model on the work it produces alone. But in real workplaces, models often help a worker rather than replace them. This benchmark measures both: across seven professional tasks, each model either solves the task directly (automation) or writes a scaffold for a fixed lower-capability worker that produces the final output (augmentation). Holding the worker constant isolates the value of the assistant's guidance, and rubric-guided judges then compare the final outputs through blind pairwise matchups.

Abstract

Organizations adopting generative AI increasingly face a model-selection problem that existing benchmarks do not fully answer. Most LLM benchmarks evaluate models as autonomous task solvers, scoring the quality of a model's final output. Yet in workplaces, models are often used not in isolation but to assist a human worker by structuring the problem, surfacing constraints, or improving the worker's reasoning. In these settings, the economically relevant question is not only which model can complete a task, but which model can improve the work of another agent.

We introduce a framework for evaluating models across two regimes: automation, in which a model completes a task end-to-end, and augmentation, where a focal model provides a scaffold that is passed to a fixed lower-capability worker model, which then produces the final output. Holding the worker constant isolates the value of the assistant's guidance. We apply this framework to seven economically grounded professional tasks spanning structured planning, analytical work, and human-facing services. Outputs are evaluated using task-specific and general rubrics through blind pairwise comparisons by multiple LLM judges, with rankings replicated across three independent runs.

Our results show that automation and augmentation produce meaningfully different model rankings. Automation rankings are more concentrated and more directionally aligned with conventional model capability orderings, while augmentation rankings are more fragmented and task-dependent. No single model dominates across all augmentation tasks, and strong direct task solvers are not always the strongest assistants. These findings have direct implications for model selection, human–AI collaboration, and multi-agent delegation: organizations and researchers should benchmark not only whether models can do work, but also whether they can improve the work of others.

Criterion-Validity Checks

These checks reuse the existing automation outputs. They ask whether held-out cross-family judges recover expected within-family capability gradients.

Validation Design

GPT ladder. Claude-Opus-4.8 judges GPT-family automation outputs: GPT-3.5-Turbo, O3-Mini, O4-Mini, GPT-4.1, and GPT-5-Mini.

Claude pair. GPT-4.1 judges Claude-family automation outputs: Claude-Sonnet-4.6 and Claude-Opus-4.8.

Held-out judge. The judge never belongs to the model family being validated, reducing same-family preference.

Interpretation. Higher expected-order agreement means the rubric/judge pipeline more often recovers the anticipated within-family ordering.

Why no Gemini or DeepSeek ladder. These criterion checks rely on a known within-family capability gradient (several models of increasing strength from the same family). Our roster includes only a single Gemini model (Gemini-3.1-Pro) and a single DeepSeek model (DeepSeek-V3.1), so there is no within-family ordering to recover and no validation ladder can be built for them.

How To Read Judge Agreement

Spearman. Rank correlation between two judges. Higher means they order models more similarly; 1.0 would be identical ranking.

Within one rank. Share of shared model-task entries where two judges place the model no more than one rank apart.

Mean gap. Average absolute rank difference. Lower means closer agreement.
